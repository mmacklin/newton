Dipper Static Sag Experiment

Generated 20260505T030209Z. The piston is held at zero stroke in all runs. The measurement is the distance between the elastic tip attachment and the same point on the undeformed floating frame, so it isolates modal bending from rigid-frame motion.

Question

The observed dipper sag grows slowly even when the piston is fixed. This experiment checks whether that is caused by timestep size, modal mass/damping, beam stiffness, adaptive joint penalties, or convergence of the split rigid/elastic VBD coupling.

Method

  • Freeze the prismatic actuator at the horizontal configuration.
  • Measure only modal tip bend, not rigid-frame rotation.
  • Use a zero-gravity control to rule out numerical drift without load.
  • Sweep VBD iterations with normal adaptive joint penalties and with joint penalties pinned to their caps.
  • Sweep fixed joint stiffness at high iteration count to compare sag, joint error, and the penalty-reaction proxy k*C.
  • Sweep substeps, primary bending stiffness, modal damping, and modal mass.
  • Fit a first-order response to measured tip bend only as a compact rate diagnostic; the fit is not assumed to be the governing model.

Finding: the sag disappears with zero gravity and is nearly insensitive to substepping, so this is not free numerical drift or a timestep artifact. Modal mass and damping have little effect on the early response, so it is not primarily a modal oscillator settling slowly. The iteration sweeps are non-monotonic: with adaptive penalties the sag peaks around the mid-iteration range, while with fixed penalties high iteration counts drive the residual down and almost eliminate modal deflection. The fixed joint stiffness sweep shows the important failure mode: joint error drops roughly with stiffness and k*C stays in the same broad force range, but sag changes dramatically. This points to the current residual-driven split rigid/elastic coupling as the dominant issue: the elastic update is seeing constraint residual leakage, not the true converged joint reaction.
